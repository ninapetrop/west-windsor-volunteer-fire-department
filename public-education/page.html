Carbon Monoxide?

Carbon monoxide (CO) is a colorless, odorless, and tasteless gas that can be deadly. It is produced whenever fuels such as gas, oil, wood, or charcoal are burned. Common household appliances, like furnaces, stoves, water heaters, and fireplaces, can produce CO if not properly maintained or ventilated.

Why Do Homeowners Need CO Detectors?

Without a carbon monoxide detector, you may never know when dangerous levels of this gas are present in your home. Prolonged exposure to CO can lead to symptoms like headaches, dizziness, confusion, and even death. As firefighters, we often see tragedies that could have been prevented with the simple installation of a CO detector.

How to Use and Maintain CO Detectors
  • Install detectors on every level of your home and near sleeping areas.
  • Test your detectors monthly to ensure they are working correctly.
  • Replace batteries at least once a year, or when prompted by the low-battery alarm.
  • Replace the entire unit according to the manufacturer’s guidelines, usually every 5–7 years.
  • Never ignore a CO alarm. If it sounds, evacuate your home immediately and call emergency services.
Protect Your Family and Home

Carbon monoxide detectors are one of the most affordable and effective tools for keeping your family safe from CO poisoning. As firefighters, our goal is to help prevent emergencies before they happen. Installing and maintaining CO detectors can save lives and give you peace of mind. Protect your loved ones—don’t wait until it’s too late.

Chimney and Woodstove Safety

A warm fire in the hearth or the steady heat from a woodstove can make a home feel cozy, especially during colder months. However, if not properly maintained, chimneys and woodstoves can pose serious fire and carbon monoxide risks. As firefighters, we know that most chimney fires and related incidents are preventable with proper care and attention.

  • Regular Inspections: Have your chimney and woodstove inspected by a certified professional at least once a year. Inspections can identify damage, blockages, or creosote buildup that could lead to a fire.
  • Keep It Clean: Chimneys should be cleaned annually or more often if you use your fireplace or woodstove frequently. Creosote, a byproduct of burning wood, is highly flammable and a leading cause of chimney fires.
  • Burn Properly: Only burn seasoned, dry wood to reduce creosote buildup. Avoid burning green wood, paper, or trash, as these materials can produce excess smoke and deposits.
  • Use a Screen or Glass Doors: If you have an open fireplace, use a screen or glass doors to keep sparks and embers from escaping into your home.
  • Check Your Chimney Cap: Ensure the chimney cap is in place and in good condition. A cap helps keep debris, animals, and moisture out of the chimney.
  • Install Smoke and CO Detectors: Place detectors near your fireplace or woodstove and on every level of your home. Test them regularly to ensure they are functioning properly.
Signs of Chimney Problems

Be on the lookout for warning signs like black soot around your fireplace, unusual odors, or poor ventilation. If you hear loud cracking or popping sounds while using your fireplace, it could indicate a chimney fire—exit your home immediately and call emergency services.

Firepit and Burn Pile Safety Tips

Firepits are a great way to enjoy the outdoors, but during brushfire season, they can become a serious hazard. Dry vegetation, high winds, and hot embers can quickly turn a relaxing evening into a dangerous wildfire. As firefighters, we urge everyone to prioritize safety when using firepits in high-risk seasons.

  • Check Local Regulations: Always confirm firepit use is allowed in your area during brushfire season. Some regions may issue bans or restrictions to reduce wildfire risks.
  • Choose the Right Location: Place your firepit at least 10 feet away from buildings, trees, or anything flammable. Ensure the area around the firepit is clear of dry grass, leaves, or debris.
  • Use a Screen: Cover your firepit with a spark screen to prevent embers from escaping and igniting nearby vegetation.
  • Only Burn Appropriate Materials: Use dry, seasoned wood and avoid burning trash, leaves, or other materials that can produce flying embers.
  • Never Leave the Fire Unattended: Always have someone monitoring the fire, and keep children and pets at a safe distance.
  • Keep Safety Supplies Nearby: Have a bucket of water, a garden hose, or a fire extinguisher close in case of an emergency.
  • Extinguish Thoroughly: When finished, douse the fire with water and stir the ashes to ensure everything is fully extinguished and cool to the touch.
Be Aware of Weather Conditions

Windy days significantly increase the risk of a fire spreading. Avoid using your firepit when high winds are forecasted. Always monitor the weather, and if conditions seem unsafe, wait for a calmer day.

Prevent Wildfires, Protect Your Community

Firepits can be a safe and enjoyable activity when used responsibly, even during brushfire season. By following these safety guidelines, you help reduce the risk of accidental fires that can endanger lives, property, and natural habitats. As firefighters, we thank you for doing your part to keep our community safe.
